Carbon Tax in Canada

Background

Scientists have been well aware of global warming for several years. A United Nations Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change report in 2007 warned that artificial greenhouse gas emissions are the likely cause of global warming, which was supported by over 2000 climate scientists, including Canadians. Climate scientists predict that Canada will be especially impacted due to its cold weather. Canada has 9% of the global renewable water supply, with nearly 900,000 square kilometres of water. Climate change would affect Canadaâ€™s geography if this water were to disappear, and prairies could experience drought and forest fires.

Canada's response

Canada signed the Kyoto Protocol in 2002, a treaty between countries to reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions to 6% below 1990 levels by 2012. The 2006 Clean Air Act implemented under the Harper government created GHG emission regulations on industries, including the automobile industry. The government also reduced taxes when using a transit pass, to encourage the use of public transportation. However, the Harper government was against the Kyoto Protocol, as they claimed it would affect the economy by limiting the use of GHGs - they decided to back out of the treaty in 2011. Justin Trudeau was the first prime minister to act on the climate change issue, promising that Canada would play an international leadership role at COP21.

Greenhouse Gas Pollution Pricing Act

On June 21, 2018, the federal Greenhouse Gas Pollution Pricing Act was implemented. The first part of the act imposes taxes on fuels for distributors, while the second part taxes large companies that exceed emissions limits. However, this tax only applies if provinces and territories do not develop their own taxation plan. Alberta, Saskatchewan, and Ontario asked their appeal courts to decide if the act is constitutional, and the Alberta court decided that it is unconstitutional, while the Saskatchewan and Ontario appeal courts decided that it is.

Peace, Order, and good Government

Section 91 of the Constitution Act discusses what heads of power (specific areas) the federal government can make laws on, and section 92 similarly discusses heads of power for provincial governments. A division of powers problem occurs when it is not clear which government has the authority to enact laws in a certain area - in this case, climate change was not an issue when the Constitution was written, therefore a division of powers problem occurred.

Gap branch

If the writers of the constitution did not consider a head of power, this branch justifies the federal government being given it if the writers would have obviously given it to them.

National concern branch

This branch allows the federal government to impose laws on a head of power if it is of national concern.

Emergency branch

This branch gives the federal government the power to make temporary laws in times of emergency.
